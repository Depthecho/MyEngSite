{% translate "Phrasal Verbs" %}

{% translate "Phrasal verbs are combinations of a main verb and one or two particles (prepositions or adverbs) that form a new meaning, often quite different from the individual words. They are an essential part of everyday spoken and informal English." %}

{% translate "Understanding phrasal verbs is crucial for fluent communication, as they are used constantly by native speakers. Their meanings can sometimes be deduced from context, but often they need to be learned as distinct vocabulary items." %}

{% translate "Types of Phrasal Verbs" %}

{% translate "Phrasal verbs can be categorized based on their grammatical structure and whether they can take an object, and if so, whether the object can be separated from the verb and particle(s)." %}

  • {% translate "Transitive vs. Intransitive:" %}
    • {% translate "Transitive:" %} {% translate "Take a direct object (e.g.," %} pick up the book).
    • {% translate "Intransitive:" %} {% translate "Do not take a direct object (e.g.," %} break down - {% translate "\"The car broke down.\"" %}).
  • {% translate "Separable vs. Inseparable:" %}
    • {% translate "Separable:" %} {% translate "The object can come between the verb and the particle, especially if the object is a pronoun (e.g.," %} turn off the light / turn the light off / turn it off).
    • {% translate "Inseparable:" %} {% translate "The object must come after the particle (e.g.," %} look after the baby / look after her - *{% translate "not" %}* {% translate "\"look the baby after\"" %}).

{% translate "Learning phrasal verbs often involves memorizing not just their meaning but also their grammatical behavior (whether they are transitive/intransitive and separable/inseparable)." %}
