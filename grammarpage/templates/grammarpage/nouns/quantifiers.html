{% translate "Quantifiers" %}

{% translate "Quantifiers are words that are used before a noun to express a quantity or amount of something. They answer the question 'how much?' or 'how many?'" %}

{% translate "Quantifiers for Countable and Uncountable Nouns" %}

{% translate "Quantifiers are often categorized based on whether they are used with countable nouns (things you can count, e.g., 'books') or uncountable nouns (things you cannot count, e.g., 'water')." %}

{% translate "Common Quantifiers in Detail" %}

{% translate "Much vs. Many" %}

  • Much is used with uncountable nouns.
    I don't have much money.
  • Many is used with countable nouns.
    There are many cars on the road.

{% translate "A few vs. A little" %}

  • A few is used with countable nouns, indicating a small number.
    I have a few friends in the city.
  • A little is used with uncountable nouns, indicating a small amount.
    He added a little sugar to his coffee.

{% translate "Some vs. Any" %}

  • Some is used in affirmative sentences for both countable and uncountable nouns.
    I have some apples. We need some water.
  • Any is typically used in negative sentences and questions.
    Do you have any questions? I don't have any money.
